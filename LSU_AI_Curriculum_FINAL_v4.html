Why This Curriculum Exists: The Never-Skilling Problem

Before any implementation detail, this is the clinical education problem that every other decision in this document is trying to solve.

The risk is not that AI will replace clinical reasoning. It is that students who use AI before they have built clinical reasoning may never build it. Ke et al. (Nature Medicine, 2026) call this never-skilling — and it is distinct from anything medical education has confronted before.

When AI systems deliver correct answers during the developmental window when clinical schemas are forming, they remove the effortful cognitive processing that schema formation requires. The student who uses AI to generate a differential on day one of M1 does not practice the reasoning that would build the neural architecture for independent diagnosis. Two years later, that student may perform adequately on AI-assisted assessments and score poorly on AI-restricted licensing exams. The gap is not detectable until it is too late to close.

Important caveat: Direct causal evidence from clinical trainees does not yet exist. The concern is grounded in desirable difficulties theory (Bjork), cognitive load theory, and early empirical signals from nonclinical populations — including a randomized study showing a 17% reduction in unaided math scores after unrestricted AI tutor access (Bastani et al., cited in Ke et al. 2026). This framework is precautionary, not evidence-established.

Three Distinct Competency Risks — One Curriculum Addresses All Three

Understanding the difference matters because the educational intervention for each is different.

ConceptDefinitionWho Is at RiskHow It AppearsWhy It Is DangerousCurriculum Response
Never-Skilling
Primary M1–M2 Risk		 Failure to develop foundational clinical reasoning competencies during formative training because AI substitutes the cognitive effort those competencies require. Medical students M1–M2. Affects all trainees equally. Disproportionate impact on those with lower baseline clinical exposure. Cannot generate differential without AI prompting. Fails AI-restricted exams. Confidently incorrect when AI is unavailable. Insidious. Masked by high performance on AI-assisted assessments. Undetectable until Step 1 or residency entry. AI-free proctored baseline (M1–M2). Standalone gate OSCE before clerkships. No AI in answer-delivery mode during assessed tasks.
De-Skilling
Primary GME Risk		 Erosion of established clinical competencies in trained clinicians due to prolonged AI reliance. The cognitive structures exist but atrophy from disuse. Experienced residents and attending physicians who have already built clinical reasoning but increasingly outsource it to AI tools. Slower unaided diagnosis. Lower polyp detection rates when AI removed (gastroenterology evidence). Reduced independent radiology interpretation. Measurable against a known historical baseline. Detectable earlier than never-skilling but reversible only with structured re-exposure. Staged AI exposure model (PGY1 minimal → PGY2 graduated). Annual AI-restricted assessment for IM and EM residents. De-skilling surveillance built into program evaluation.
Mis-Skilling
M3–M4 Risk		 Acquisition of incorrect clinical reasoning patterns through uncritical adoption of biased or erroneous AI outputs. The student builds schemas — but builds them wrong. Students in M3–M4 clerkships with unmonitored access to AI-generated clinical documentation and differentials. Confidently incorrect differentials aligned with AI bias (e.g., ACS missed in women because AI anchored to musculoskeletal). Internalized race-based diagnostic errors. The most difficult to detect. Student appears clinically confident. Errors are systematic, not random. Can persist into practice. Adversarial AI debugging workshops (M2 and M3). Dual-Reasoning Reflection Log mandates independent reasoning before AI display. RAG audit trains source verification.

Memorization → Clinical Reasoning

M1: Recall facts reliably
↓
M2–M3: Apply facts to patient scenarios independently
This shift must happen before AI enters in answer-delivery mode. If AI gets there first, the schemas never form. That is the never-skilling window — and the AI-free gate is the only structural protection we have.
📖→🏥

Classroom → Bedside

M1–M2: Lecture halls and labs
↓
M3: Real patients, real uncertainty, AI tools present
The gate OSCE ensures students arrive at the bedside with documented AI-independent reasoning. Without it, students who learned alongside AI in M1–M2 have no verified baseline — and neither does the institution.
❌🤖→✓🤖

AI Prohibited → AI Supervised

M1–M2: AI forbidden in assessed tasks
↓
M3: AI permitted only with completed DRRL
The DRRL is not paperwork. It is the only mechanism that produces a timestamped record of what the student thought before they saw what the AI thought. Without it, there is no evidence of reasoning — only evidence of a finished note.
📝→⚡

Writing Notes → Auditing AI Drafts

M2: Writes standard clinical notes
↓
M3: Audits AI draft, flags errors, appends disclosure log
AI documentation tools are already in EHRs. Students are already using them — with or without training. The discharge summary safety audit makes error-detection a graded clinical skill rather than an accidental discovery.
🏥→🏛️

M3: Managing this patient's problem
↓
M4: Evaluating AI tools that affect every patient
The Mock Governance Board is not a simulation of leadership. It is the beginning of it. A physician who cannot evaluate an AI tool at the system level cannot lead a department or sit on a hospital committee in 2030.

GME Integration & Faculty Development

Internal Medicine and Emergency Medicine residents in PGY1–PGY2. Faculty train-the-trainer program built from scratch through a new AI Education Working Group.

PGY1–PGY2 · Internal Medicine

IM Resident AI Milestones

Highest volume of AI-influenced clinical decisions. First GME pilot site.

Daily Rounds — AI Disclosure Statement Verbalize independent assessment before AI recommendation is displayed to the team. Document AI Disclosure Statement (≤2 sentences) in ≥95% of daily notes during any 2-week audit. ≥1 meaningful override per week with attending-validated reasoning. Zero-override across ≥10 cases triggers mandatory attending debrief — treated as a teaching moment, not a disciplinary one.
Medication Reconciliation Safety Log Obtain independent medication history before viewing AI-reconciled list. Document ≥1 discrepancy or explicit concordance statement. High-risk drugs (anticoagulants, insulin, narrow-TI agents): escalate to attending pharmacist within 30 minutes. 100% of admits with ≥5 medications during any 30-day audit window.
AI Governance Committee Participation ≥2 committee meetings per year. ≥1 Responsible Use Checklist completed for a tool used on their service. ≥1 QI case presentation: real AI error, patient impact, corrective action, system mitigation. Faculty scoring ≥3/4. Documents ACGME SBP-4 milestone.
Annual AI-Independent Assessment (De-Skilling Surveillance) Annual AI-restricted case assessment for all IM residents. Results are confidential and used for educational planning only — not as performance evaluation. Tracks whether AI-assisted training is eroding independent diagnostic accuracy over time. Documented for GME program evaluation.
PGY1–PGY2 · Emergency Medicine

EM Resident AI Milestones

Highest AI alert density. Sepsis models, deterioration scores, and triage algorithms are live in the EM workflow.

Sepsis Alert Escalation Protocol (15-Minute Hard Stop) On receiving a CDS sepsis alert: document independent Sepsis-3 assessment within 15 minutes (HR, RR, temp, WBC, lactate, mental status, BP). Estimate personal probability (low/medium/high) and intended action. If assessment conflicts with alert severity, notify attending within 10 minutes and document conflict explicitly. No management action based on AI alert without bedside assessment.
Triage AI Audit For AI-assisted triage recommendations: document independent acuity assessment before viewing AI suggestion. If AI triage level differs from clinical assessment by ≥1 level, document rationale for accepted or overridden recommendation. 30-day audit of concordance rates reviewed by EM program director.
Alert Fatigue Documentation Residents track and report instances where AI alert frequency contributed to a near-miss or inappropriate dismissal of a valid alert. Reports submitted through existing patient safety infrastructure. Aggregated quarterly for program director review. Equity lens: sepsis model may under-alert for patients with atypical presentations — residents briefed at orientation.
AI-Restricted Resuscitation Drill (Annual) Annual simulation where AI decision support tools are deliberately disabled. Residents demonstrate independent resuscitation decision-making (ACLS algorithms, medication calculations, differential generation) without AI assistance. Ensures clinical resilience when technology fails.

Faculty Development — AI Education Working Group (Built from Scratch)

The faculty gap is the biggest implementation risk Nationally, approximately 9% of medical faculty report meaningful AI expertise (Ke et al. 2026 — estimate; LSU figure unknown). Faculty do not need to become AI engineers. They need three things: a clinically validated case, a clear rubric, and one 30-minute calibration session. The train-the-trainer program builds exactly this — and nothing more.

AI Education Working Group Structure

Not a committee. A small, action-oriented group that designs, validates, and evaluates the pilot. Built in parallel with the Faculty Development Office — not through it.

Lead: Proposal author — design, mapping, evaluation, CQI dashboard
UME: Associate Dean for UME / Curriculum Committee representative — curricular approval and clerkship fit
GME: DIO or GME education leader — resident readiness and EPA linkage
Clinical: IM clerkship director + EM program director — case validation and site oversight
Compliance: Privacy / IT / compliance officer — tool boundaries and PHI rules
Student: Two MS3 or MS4 representatives — feasibility feedback and student voice

Target: 3 Faculty Per Department — What They Need to Do

The train-the-trainer program certifies faculty to run three specific tasks — nothing broader.

Task 1: Run an adversarial AI debugging session Select from library of pre-validated cases. Facilitate student critique. Score Workshop Error Reports against standardized rubric. Time burden: ≤30 min per session.
Task 2: Review and countersign DRRLs Read independent plan, review DRRL, confirm that reasoning preceded AI comparison. Time burden: ≤15 min per DRRL. Minimum one per student per week in IM pilot.
Task 3: Apply the DEFT-AI supervisory framework When observing incidental AI use: Diagnosis (what was used), Evidence (how output was verified), Feedback (on AI engagement), Teaching (appropriate use), Recommendation (future guidance). Time burden: 5 min per encounter.

The 15-Minute Meeting Language

The framing that works in a dean-level conversation. Use this verbatim or adapt it.

"AI is entering clinical care and medical education faster than curricula can adapt. The issue is not whether students will use AI. It is whether they will use it safely, transparently, and with preserved clinical reasoning."
"The central educational risk is never-skilling. If students use AI too early, they look competent before building reasoning. This curriculum uses a reasoning-first, AI-second model — protecting schemas before integrating AI."
"MS1 builds foundations. MS2 uses adversarial error detection. MS3 uses dual-reasoning logs in the IM clerkship. MS4 uses a governance capstone. IM and EM residents extend this into supervised clinical practice."
"This is not a side project. It creates objectives, assessment evidence, clerkship comparability, faculty oversight, and CQI data — the things LCME actually looks for."
"I am not asking LSU to launch a 4-year AI curriculum today. I am asking for permission to develop a 90-day UME-GME pilot proposal and bring it to Robin and Lee for feedback. Three touchpoints. No PHI. No live EHR AI. No new vendor. Formative assessment first."

Establish the problem. AI is in the building already. The question is whether students are prepared for it.

5 Practical Planning Tips

From the evidence base — what has worked and what has not, across the institutions that moved first.

1

Lead with the policy LSU already has

LSU's generative AI policy is the institutional anchor. Do not present this as starting from zero. Present it as: "We have the guardrails. This is the curriculum that operationalizes them." That argument is far stronger than anything borrowed from Stanford or UCSF.

2

Make the DRRL the institutional signature

The Dual-Reasoning Reflection Log is concrete, measurable, and explainable in one sentence. It is also what distinguishes LSU from schools that merely "teach AI." Make it the thing LSU becomes known for — not the curriculum itself, but the specific artifact it produces.

3

Pilot IM first — it is the right test site

Internal Medicine has the highest volume of admits, the longest rotation, the most consistent attending presence for DRRL countersignature, and the most AI alert exposure. One 6-week IM pilot generates more usable data than a scattered multi-site rollout. Prove it there, then scale on evidence.

4

Never let PHI touch a commercial LLM

The adversarial AI case, the governance board, and the AI literacy module all work entirely with synthetic or de-identified cases. This eliminates the compliance risk that has stalled AI curriculum proposals at other institutions. If the pilot stays synthetic, the IRB pathway for scholarship is also straightforward.

5

Build the CQI archive from session one

LCME rewards documented improvement cycles, not good intentions. From the first DRRL review, log completion rates, faculty time, and site comparability. This data becomes your self-study evidence and your argument for scale. The curriculum that can show its own outcomes is the curriculum that survives leadership changes.

Dean's Institutional Readiness Assessment

Synopsis tool — for meeting preparation. Twelve questions across four domains. Each shows why it matters at LSU. Answer all questions and click the button to generate your readiness score and a prioritized action plan to take to Robin and Lee.
Domain A — UME Preclinical (MS1–MS2) · Q1–Q3
Q1. Are MS1–MS2 assessed tasks conducted in AI-free environments?
Why this matters at LSUSchema formation is interrupted when AI delivers answers during the MS1–MS2 developmental window. This is the primary never-skilling mechanism (Ke et al., Nature Medicine 2026). This period is the only one where AI-free assessment has meaningful protective effect on durable clinical reasoning.
Q2. Is there a formal AI literacy module in the M1 curriculum?
Why this matters at LSUHunt et al. (npj Digital Medicine 2026) — 54 studies — found AI literacy is the most proposed and least implemented UME competency domain. A student who cannot explain hallucination, RAG source failure, or demographic bias cannot safely evaluate any AI clinical recommendation.
Q3. Does LSU have a standalone AI-free gate OSCE before M3 clerkship entry?
Why this matters at LSUNo existing end-of-M2 OSCE at LSU currently serves this function. Without it, a student can score adequately on Step 1 while being unable to generate a differential in an AI-restricted environment — and LSU has no way of knowing until residency entry.
Domain B — Clinical Clerkships (MS3–MS4) · Q4–Q5
Q4. Are Dual-Reasoning Reflection Logs required in any LSU clerkship?
Why this matters at LSUWithout a DRRL, there is no audit trail proving the student reasoned before seeing the AI recommendation. A disclosure statement shows AI was used — it does not show independent reasoning happened first. The difference between those two things is exactly what never-skilling looks like from outside.
Q5. Are adversarial AI error-detection workshops in the clerkship curriculum?
Why this matters at LSUAI errors in real clinical settings are unlabeled and plausible. Students who have only seen AI succeed will accept errors they cannot detect. Adversarial training is the DEFT-AI framework prerequisite for safe supervised AI integration (Abdulnour et al. NEJM 2025).
Domain C — GME: IM + EM Residents · Q6–Q7
Q6. Do IM and EM residents document assessments before EHR AI alerts influence their plans?
Why this matters at LSUEHR CDS tools operate in answer-delivery mode — the same mode that produces never-skilling in MS1–MS2. Without a timestamped independent assessment before AI output appears, there is no audit trail. This is the GME version of the DRRL problem.
Q7. Is there a formal escalation protocol when a resident's assessment conflicts with an AI alert?
Why this matters at LSUWithout a defined escalation pathway with a time limit, residents default to uncritical acceptance or silent override. The 15-minute bedside assessment hard stop prevents remote action on AI alerts and creates the patient safety record that ACGME SBP-4 requires.
Domain D — Policy, Faculty & Governance · Q8–Q12
Q8. Has LSU updated its academic integrity policy with explicit AI provisions by training phase?
Why this matters at LSULSU already has a generative AI policy. But without phase-specific rules and explicit enforcement mechanisms, the AI-free gate assessments have no teeth. Most handbooks predate LLMs.
Q9. Is there a written privacy policy for student and resident use of commercial LLMs with patient data?
Why this matters at LSUA student pasting patient data into ChatGPT is a HIPAA breach today regardless of intent. LSU's existing AI policy is the foundation but needs specific LLM language: which tools are approved, which are prohibited, and the breach escalation procedure (AAMC v2.0, July 2025).
Q10. Has LSU mapped AI curriculum to LCME Elements 6.3, 7.1, 7.4, 7.6, and 7.9?
Why this matters at LSULCME has not mandated AI competencies yet (AY 2025–26). But Elements 7.4, 7.6, and 7.9 directly encompass what this curriculum addresses. Proactive mapping means LSU leads when a formal AI standard arrives. Must be validated by the LCME liaison before use in self-study documentation.
Q11. Is there a named curricular owner for each of the three pilot phases?
Why this matters at LSUNamed ownership is the single most critical success factor. Only 16.7% of published AI curriculum proposals moved to implementation (Hunt et al. 2026). The difference between a launched pilot and a permanent proposal is almost always a named person with accountability.
Q12. Has compliance and IT confirmed the tool boundaries for the 90-day pilot?
Why this matters at LSUThe pilot uses synthetic or de-identified cases only — no commercial LLMs with PHI, no live EHR AI. Compliance and IT must confirm these boundaries before Session 1. A single PHI incident would halt the program. Pre-approval takes one meeting.
